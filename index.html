Understanding Distributed Systems: A Primer

By Dr. Elena Vasquez · Published March 2026 · 18 min read

Distributed systems form the backbone of modern internet infrastructure. From search engines to social networks, nearly every large-scale application relies on distributing computation across multiple machines. This primer covers the essential concepts every engineer should understand when designing systems that must scale, remain available, and tolerate failure.

1. Why Distributed Systems?

The fundamental motivation for distributed systems is simple: no single machine can handle the demands of modern applications. Google processes over 99,000 search queries per second. Netflix streams to 260 million subscribers simultaneously. These workloads require thousands of machines working in concert.

Beyond raw scale, distributed systems provide fault tolerance. When a single server fails—and servers fail constantly at scale—the system must continue operating. Amazon's internal studies showed that even a 100ms increase in latency costs 1% of sales. Downtime is measured not in inconvenience but in revenue.

The shift to cloud computing has made distributed systems the default rather than the exception. Whether you're running on AWS, GCP, or Azure, your application is inherently distributed across availability zones, regions, and service boundaries.

2. The CAP Theorem

The CAP theorem, proposed by Eric Brewer at UC Berkeley in 2000 and formally proved by Seth Gilbert and Nancy Lynch at MIT in 2002, states that a distributed system can only provide two of three guarantees simultaneously:

  • Consistency — Every read receives the most recent write or an error
  • Availability — Every request receives a non-error response, without guarantee of the most recent write
  • Partition tolerance — The system continues operating despite network partitions between nodes

In practice, since network partitions are unavoidable in distributed systems, the real choice is between consistency and availability during a partition event. This is sometimes called the "CP vs AP" decision.

CP Systems

Systems like Google Spanner, Apache ZooKeeper, and etcd prioritize consistency. During a partition, these systems may refuse to respond rather than return stale data. This is appropriate for financial transactions, distributed locks, and leader election.

AP Systems

Systems like Amazon DynamoDB, Apache Cassandra, and Riak prioritize availability. During a partition, these systems return the best available data, which may be stale. They rely on eventual consistency to reconcile divergent state after the partition heals.

It's worth noting that the CAP theorem applies only during partition events. When the network is functioning normally, a well-designed system can provide both consistency and availability. Martin Kleppmann has argued that the CAP theorem, while theoretically important, is often misapplied in practice and that the real-world tradeoffs are more nuanced than a simple two-of-three choice.

3. Consensus Protocols

Consensus protocols allow distributed nodes to agree on a single value even when some nodes fail. This is one of the most fundamental problems in distributed computing, formalized by the FLP impossibility result (Fischer, Lynch, and Paterson, 1985), which proved that no deterministic consensus protocol can guarantee progress in an asynchronous system with even one faulty process.

Paxos

Leslie Lamport's Paxos protocol (1989, published 1998) was the first practical consensus algorithm. It works through a series of proposals and acceptances, ensuring that once a value is chosen, all future proposals will choose the same value. Despite its theoretical elegance, Paxos is notoriously difficult to implement correctly. Google's Chubby lock service is perhaps the most famous production Paxos implementation.

Raft

Raft, designed by Diego Ongaro and John Ousterhout at Stanford in 2014, has become the de facto standard for consensus due to its relative simplicity. It decomposes consensus into three sub-problems: leader election, log replication, and safety. This decomposition makes it significantly easier to implement and reason about than classical Paxos.

Raft is used in production by etcd (Kubernetes' backing store), CockroachDB, TiKV, and Consul. Its understandability has been validated by user studies showing that students learn Raft significantly faster than Paxos.

Byzantine Fault Tolerance

Both Paxos and Raft assume crash failures—nodes either work correctly or stop completely. Byzantine fault tolerance (BFT) handles arbitrary failures, including malicious nodes that send conflicting information. PBFT (Practical Byzantine Fault Tolerance), proposed by Castro and Liskov in 1999, requires 3f+1 nodes to tolerate f Byzantine faults. BFT is primarily used in blockchain systems and high-security environments.

4. Data Replication Strategies

Replication is the practice of maintaining copies of data on multiple machines. It serves two purposes: fault tolerance (surviving machine failures) and performance (serving reads from the nearest replica).

StrategyConsistencyLatencyUse Case
Synchronous replicationStrongHighFinancial systems
Semi-synchronousBounded stalenessMediumMost databases
Asynchronous replicationEventualLowCDNs, caches
Quorum-basedTunableTunableDynamoDB, Cassandra

The quorum approach is particularly elegant: with N replicas, requiring W writes and R reads where W + R > N ensures that any read will see the latest write. DynamoDB defaults to N=3, W=2, R=2, but allows applications to tune these values per-request.

5. Partitioning and Sharding

When a dataset exceeds the capacity of a single machine, it must be partitioned (or sharded) across multiple nodes. The two main approaches are:

  • Range partitioning — Assigns contiguous ranges of keys to each shard. Good for range queries but prone to hotspots if access patterns cluster around certain ranges.
  • Hash partitioning — Applies a hash function to the key and assigns based on hash value. Distributes load evenly but makes range queries expensive.

Consistent hashing, introduced by Karger et al. in 1997, is a technique that minimizes data movement when nodes join or leave. Instead of rehashing all keys (which would require moving most data), consistent hashing only moves keys that map to the changed portion of the hash ring. This is used by DynamoDB, Cassandra, and Memcached.

6. Event Sourcing and CQRS

Event sourcing captures all changes to application state as a sequence of immutable events. Instead of storing the current state, the system stores the full history of state changes. This enables powerful capabilities:

  • Complete audit trails for compliance and debugging
  • Temporal queries ("what was the account balance on March 15?")
  • Event replay for rebuilding projections or debugging
  • Easy integration with stream processing systems

Command Query Responsibility Segregation (CQRS) separates read and write models, allowing each to be optimized independently. The write model validates and persists events, while read models (projections) are materialized views optimized for specific query patterns.

"The key insight of event sourcing is that state is derived, not stored. The event log is the source of truth, and any view of current state is just a cached computation over that log." — Greg Young, creator of the CQRS/ES pattern

When combined with event sourcing, CQRS enables highly scalable architectures where read replicas can be rebuilt from the event log at any time. Apache Kafka, Amazon Kinesis, and Pulsar are commonly used as the durable event log in these architectures.

7. The Saga Pattern

The saga pattern, originally described by Garcia-Molina and Salem in 1987, manages distributed transactions by breaking them into a series of local transactions, each with a compensating action. This approach avoids the performance overhead and availability problems of two-phase commit while maintaining eventual consistency.

Consider an e-commerce order: (1) reserve inventory, (2) charge payment, (3) ship order. If payment fails, the saga executes compensating actions in reverse: unreserve inventory. If shipping fails: refund payment, then unreserve inventory.

Choreography vs Orchestration

Choreography-based sagas use events to coordinate between services—each service listens for events and decides what to do next. This is loosely coupled but can become difficult to understand and debug as the number of steps grows.

Orchestration-based sagas use a central coordinator that explicitly tells each service what to do. This is easier to understand and modify but introduces a single point of coordination (though not necessarily failure, if the orchestrator itself is replicated).

8. Observability

Observability is the ability to understand the internal state of a system from its external outputs. In distributed systems, where failures are partial and non-deterministic, observability is essential for diagnosing problems.

The three pillars of observability are:

  • Metrics — Numeric measurements aggregated over time (Prometheus, Datadog, CloudWatch)
  • Logs — Structured records of discrete events (ELK stack, Splunk, Loki)
  • Traces — Records of request flows across service boundaries (Jaeger, Zipkin, AWS X-Ray)

Distributed tracing is particularly valuable because it shows the complete path of a request through multiple services. OpenTelemetry has emerged as the standard for instrumenting applications, providing vendor-neutral APIs for all three pillars.

9. Practical Challenges

Network partitions, clock skew, and Byzantine failures make distributed systems inherently complex. Peter Deutsch's "Eight Fallacies of Distributed Computing" (1994) remains relevant: developers often incorrectly assume the network is reliable, latency is zero, bandwidth is infinite, and the network is secure.

Clock synchronization deserves special attention. Physical clocks on different machines inevitably drift apart. NTP typically achieves accuracy within tens of milliseconds, which is insufficient for ordering events in a distributed system. Logical clocks (Lamport timestamps, vector clocks) and hybrid logical clocks (HLC) provide ordering guarantees independent of physical time.

Google's TrueTime API, used by Spanner, takes a different approach: it uses GPS receivers and atomic clocks to provide a bounded uncertainty interval for the current time, allowing Spanner to implement serializable transactions at global scale.

10. Emerging Trends

Several trends are reshaping distributed systems in 2026:

  • Serverless computing — AWS Lambda, Cloudflare Workers, and similar platforms abstract away server management entirely, but introduce new challenges around cold starts, state management, and debugging.
  • Edge computing — Moving computation closer to users reduces latency but complicates consistency. CRDTs (Conflict-free Replicated Data Types) are gaining adoption for edge-first architectures.
  • AI-assisted operations — LLMs are being used for log analysis, anomaly detection, and even automated incident response, though their reliability for critical decisions remains debated.
  • eBPF — Extended Berkeley Packet Filter allows running sandboxed programs in the Linux kernel, enabling unprecedented observability and networking capabilities without kernel modifications.

Conclusion

Distributed systems are challenging but increasingly necessary. Understanding these foundational concepts—the CAP theorem, consensus protocols, replication strategies, partitioning, event sourcing, sagas, and observability—provides the vocabulary and mental models needed to design resilient, scalable architectures. As Leslie Lamport famously noted: "A distributed system is one in which the failure of a computer you didn't even know existed can render your own computer unusable."

The field continues to evolve rapidly. New consensus algorithms, programming models, and infrastructure abstractions emerge regularly. But the fundamental challenges—managing state across unreliable networks, reasoning about concurrent operations, and designing for partial failure—remain constant. Master these fundamentals, and you'll be well-equipped to navigate whatever the field produces next.
